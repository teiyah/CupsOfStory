Let me tell you a story about Mr. Eeky-Weeky and his uncanny ability to make things out of fuzz.

He was a small thing, that Mr. Eeky-Weeky, yet charming as can be. A little push is all it took for Mr. Eeky-Weeky to rise above his humdrum job to his multi-billion business named Fuzz It All.

Let's go back a little though, to a place where he once needed. A place real small, where he was born, not that tall, but he called it home.

"Fresh baked cookies and a coffee pot, the smell of fresh croissants. Not quite a breakfast of champions, but close enough", he thought.

On one sunny day, as breakfast came and went, he thought "I think the day's too clear!" He jumped up high, with a half impressed smile, then sat back down to think. "I think, I think" but nothing came. He thought again, "I think, I think," and still the same. His brain a door that wouldn't open, even for the best of his mindful voices.

Another day would come and go until 100 days later, he finally spoke:

"A day too clear needs a fuzzy reinvention, an unconventional decor. It needs the sound of fuzz to ring soundtracks at passersbys, playing music at romantic restaurants. It needs curly dresses that twirl beneath the seams at the hips, fuzzy love on the skins of those who wear it.

It needs an oil painting across the river created by a master, capturing the fuzzy houses in the reflection of the water as the photos taken of the passing cars capture forever the insanity of speed with a fuzz-like exposure."

A fuzzy idea. Not very polished, but enough, he thought, to make a leash for puppy Olie he loved too much for words to... "Ah, I lost it."

He leaned down to find the wind was blowing from underneath the table he was hard at working. And there he saw it, a pile of fuzz from little Olie, whose time for shedding was prime in its glory.

And there it started and there it went. A leash, a collar, and soon a dress, for fuzz had no limits to address. He worked and worked on laces, and hats, and on many things until, at last, he tailored a beautiful coat that made its way to runways, fashionistas, and headlines. Mr. Eeky-Weeky became a sensation overnight, and he soon became the fashion icon of Milan; the world cheered at all his creations, and only the rich could afford his chic.

His world did change like he had wanted and the clear days in the sun finally ended up fuzzy.

I am terrified of dying. So much so, anything related to death puts me in a lump sum of legs and arms shoved in a corner facing just enough wall to feel safe. I hate it.

It all started when my aunt died in her sleep due to a brain aneurysm. Wrapping my head around the thought that you can kiss someone goodnight, never to wake up to them again is close to… "Um, no”.

The fear is probably a useless one, especially because I can't control death. And yet, I find myself in tears picturing when death will come. We go through life thinking it's the enemy that ends all, takes away the you, leaves behind the remains you have no control of, and smirks at the hope that your family might have actually read the letter attached to your will and scattered you over Mount Kilimanjaro like you've always dreamed of. We reconcile with it through preparations: life insurances, wills, savings accounts, hoping to leave behind a safe haven for our loved ones.

Somewhere in this cluster of vast fear and precaution, I have forgotten I am currently very much alive - locked up in my house so that others can also have a fighting chance at life. There’s a sense of unity with the world as I sit here trying to remember what my friends used to look like.

I think it must be a life lesson of some sort.

Even when I do get in touch with friends, I have a hard time catching them up. It’s like a ping pong game of “How are yous?” and “What's news?”, both sides feeling a lack of conversation that used to be so natural. How do you talk about the internal growth, the pain, the struggles, and the setbacks when they are all brand new to you? I sometimes don’t even recognise myself through this new lens of isolation. It was easier when I could talk about my awful trips to London, or my first hangover at a gay bar where I met one of my best friends, or any story about something that actually happened.

I often think about how our generation will go down in history.

Our grandparents went to war, telling us stories of their battles. What will we tell our grandkids? That we complained about being stuck inside for a few months while the clubs shut down? That we laughed at people in power until they made us cry? That we took too long to fight for equality until one too many people died? We might just need to tell them that we were always a step behind, but always had the right intentions.

I am terrified of dying because I don't know how our story will be told.
